          Assignment Instructions

          Essay 1 (350 word minimum)
          The Session in Gleeson’s Pub

          See attachment for assignment details. 

           

                    Jimmy Gleeson is the publican of Gleeson’s pub; the pub has been in the family for four generations. In addition, both his wife, and children help him operate the pub for the music sessions. Jim Glesson established Glesson’s pub as a location for informal sessions, dance, and social meeting place in 1978 by expanding it to meet the demand. The Dance Halls Act of 1935 had made informal country, and in house sessions of traditional music and dance illegal unless it was a  licensed location; allbut ending the informal get to gethers as Jim Gleeson states. Jim Gleeson has also attracted musicians by offering free drinks and food to the musicians when they take breaks.


                    Marten(Junior) Crehen and Michael Downes were the senior players at Glesson’s pub and led the sessions as fiddle players. From the reading, I also believe Katty Hayes on concertina is also a senior player since her husband played the flute with Marten for seventy years. Each of these players have been playing there for quite some time. The newer players at Glesson’s pub are represented by Eamon McGivney, and Conor Keane. Peter Laban, Gabi and Geoff Wooff are frequent players at Glesson’s pub but not consistently.

                    The audience for the music was the local, and trusted only. After reading “Invitation to a Session” I wanted to say that it was for the commoner. But, then, I remembered that on page one, Jerry O’Reilly is acting as though the session is a well kept secret and inviting only certain people, and quickly. During the session as well, I got the feeling that the group that was there was quite close with few as the author doesn’t mention seeing a large crowd. Maybe 15 - 20 people. The audience was mostly other musicians, and dancers.

                    At the session there were fiddles, concertinas, an accordion, a tin whistle, and bagpipes.

                    Without pulling in outside resources I cannot say whether this is a traditional session or not. I believe since Marten Crehen is in attendance, and based solely off his years of traditional Irish musical experience as described by the author would make this a traditional session.
